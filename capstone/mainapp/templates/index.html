Surplus property is also known as condemned property or surplus property. It refers to a specific type of property that has been acquired by a government or entity. An organization in special circumstances, such as for public infrastructure projects. Urban development, environmental conservation or any other public purpose.

Characteristics of a surplus property: Acquisition by a Public Entity: A surplus property is property that has been acquired by a government entity or non-profit organization for the purpose of using it for a public or social project.

Acquisition Process: The acquisition of surplus properties is often involves a legal process of expropriation or voluntary purchase. In some cases, Property owners can be compensated for the loss of their property. Use for Public Projects: Surplus properties are usually used for public interest projects, such as the construction of roads, parks, schools, hospitals or other services and facilities necessary for the community.

Change of Purpose: Once a property is considered surplus, its previous use may change. For example, a plot of land that was once private it can become a public space or be reassigned for other public purposes. Regulation and Legislation: The acquisition and use of surplus property are regulated by specific laws and regulations that vary by country and local jurisdiction. These laws typically ensure that property owners receive fair compensation and that property rights are respected.
